Two scheduling changes — costing zero extra study time — reliably improve
retention: spread sessions out, and mix topics within them.

## Spacing

[[spaced-repetition|Spaced repetition]] applies the forgetting-curve lesson from chapter one:
the same four hours of study produce dramatically more retention as four
spaced sessions than as one cram. Each gap lets a little forgetting happen,
and overcoming that forgetting is what deepens the memory.

## Interleaving

[[interleaving]] mixes problem types within a session: A-B-C-A-C-B instead of
AAAA-BBBB-CCCC. Blocked practice tells you which tool to use before you
start; mixed practice makes you *pick* the tool — the exact skill exams
demand. Expect it to feel slower and messier. That difficulty is the point.

## Together

A weekly plan that rotates subjects across short, spaced, mixed sessions
quietly applies both techniques at once. The last lesson of this chapter
turns that into a concrete plan.
