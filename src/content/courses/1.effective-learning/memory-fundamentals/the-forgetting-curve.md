In the 1880s, Hermann Ebbinghaus memorized lists of nonsense syllables and
tested himself at intervals — producing the famous **forgetting curve**.

## The bad news

Forgetting is fast. Within hours of learning something new, recall starts
dropping; within days, most of an unreviewed lesson is gone. This isn't a
personal failing — it's how every human brain treats information it hasn't
been convinced to keep.

## The good news

Every review flattens the curve. And crucially, the *timing* matters: a
review right before the memory fades does far more good than one while the
material is still fresh. That insight is the whole basis of
[[spaced-repetition|spaced repetition]] — reviewing at growing intervals, each one placed where
forgetting is about to win.
