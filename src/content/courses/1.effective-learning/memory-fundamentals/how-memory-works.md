---
title: How Memory Works
---

Learning is the process of moving knowledge from a tiny, crowded workspace
into effectively unlimited long-term storage.

## Two systems

Your [[working-memory|working memory]] is where conscious thinking happens — and it can hold
only about **four chunks** of new information at a time. That overwhelmed
feeling when a topic is new? That's working memory overflowing, not a lack of
ability.

Long-term memory, by contrast, has no practical limit. Everything you
comfortably know — words, faces, skills — lives there and is pulled back into
the workspace on demand.

## What "learning" actually is

A fact is *learned* when you can retrieve it from long-term memory reliably,
not when it merely feels familiar on the page. Familiarity is the great trap
of studying: re-reading makes material feel known while leaving you unable to
reproduce any of it.

That distinction — familiarity versus retrievability — drives everything else
in this course. The techniques that feel hardest, like [[active-recall|active recall]], are
the ones that build retrievability.
